Why This Exists

Cambridge builds world-class technology. We bridge it to the world's biggest ecosystem.

The best founders in Cambridge are building world-class technology. But most have never set foot in Silicon Valley — never sat across from the people who built billion-dollar companies. Never felt the energy of a place where everyone around you is building something.

Bridge Fellows changes that in one week. We take the most ambitious founders from Cambridge to Silicon Valley for an immersion experience.

This isn't a relocation program. It's a perspective shift. We want you to see how the grass is on the other side — to experience what it actually feels like to be in the room. Then you decide where you want to build your company. Cambridge, San Francisco, or anywhere else. That's your call.

What You Get

  • Daily sessions with world-class founders, operators, and investors
  • Small group and 1:1 time with people who've built what you're dreaming of
  • Visits to high-growth companies to see how elite teams operate
  • Learn how US founders validate ideas, move fast, and raise money
  • Join an alumni community that keeps pushing you long after the week ends

Once in Your Shoes

Every billion-dollar company started with someone facing the same uncertainty you feel now. Here are four Cambridge founders who turned doubt into breakthrough companies.

Demis Hassabis

DeepMind → Google ($650M acquisition)

The Challenge: Demis had a PhD from Cambridge and wanted to start an AI company in 2010. UK investors literally rolled their eyes—"AI" was a dirty word. Most wouldn't take the meeting.

The Turning Point: No UK funding, so he flew to Silicon Valley. One dinner pitch to a tech billionaire unlocked the first few million.

The Outcome: Google acquired DeepMind for $650M in 2014. Demis won the 2024 Nobel Prize in Chemistry for AlphaFold.

Jon Reynolds & Ben Medlock

SwiftKey → Microsoft ($250M acquisition)

The Challenge: Jon studied physics at Cambridge, hated his civil service job. Ben was doing a PhD in NLP. They had an idea for a keyboard but no funding, no business experience, and one co-founder who quit after 2 months.

The Turning Point: £15,000 UK grant, built a prototype, flew to Mobile World Congress 2010. Couldn't afford a booth—walked the floor pitching. Launch day: 50,000 downloads.

The Outcome: Microsoft acquired SwiftKey for $250M in 2016. Powers billions of devices today.

Eben Upton

Raspberry Pi (IPO 2024, $1B+ valuation)

The Challenge: As Director of Studies at Cambridge, Eben watched computer science applications collapse. Spent 6 years hand-soldering prototypes in obscurity. Then a BBC video went viral—600,000 views in 2 days.

The Turning Point: Nobody made $35 computers. Manufacturing seemed impossible. Launch day: 100,000 units sold. Had to limit orders to one per customer.

The Outcome: 60M+ Raspberry Pis sold. IPO'd 2024 at $1B+ valuation. Computer Science at Cambridge became the hardest subject to get into.

Poppy Gustafsson

Darktrace → Thoma Bravo ($5.3B acquisition)

The Challenge: In 2013, Poppy was an accountant returning part-time after maternity leave. Cambridge mathematicians and GCHQ cyber experts had a radical AI idea for cybersecurity, but no one to commercialize it.

The Turning Point: Set up dual headquarters in Cambridge and San Francisco from day one. First customer: a major UK power station. By 2015, serving Fortune 100 companies globally.

The Outcome: IPO'd 2021 at £2.5B valuation. Acquired by Thoma Bravo for $5.3B in 2024. Poppy now serves as UK Minister of State for Investment.

Questions

Frequently asked

How much does it cost?

Nothing. The program is fully sponsored. Flights, accommodation, food, and all events are covered. You just show up and bring your ambition.

Do I need to have a company already?

No. You can be working on a side project, a research spin-out, or an idea you're serious about. We care about what you're building and your drive, not whether you've incorporated.

Is this a relocation program?

No. We give you a taste of Silicon Valley so you can experience the ecosystem first-hand. Where you build your company after that is entirely your decision. Cambridge, San Francisco, London — your call.

Can I apply as a team?

Yes. You can apply solo or with your co-founder(s). Just fill out one application and mention your team.

How are applications reviewed?

On a rolling basis. We read every application as it comes in. Early applicants may hear back sooner. We select up to 10 founders per cohort.

What happens during the week?

A mix of office hours with unicorn founders, company visits to leading startups and VC firms, pitch practice sessions, networking dinners, and a demo day. Every day is designed to be intense, but exceptional.

What happens after the program?

You join the Bridge Fellows alumni network — a lifelong community of ambitious Cambridge founders. We continue with introductions, quarterly check-ins, and ongoing support. The network is the long-term value.

Do I need to give equity?

No equity is required to participate. The program is free with no strings attached.
